Bolivia, Oruro: 26th October 2002

The owner from a beautiful farm called Hacianda Cayara outside Potosi (the farm we were told is 500 years old and is truly spectacular!) made sure that we were well-fed and Miss M did the same for herself whilst she was in Potosi, eating lots of healthy vegetarian food and drinking freshly squeezed juices and shakes whenever she got the opportunity. We were rather worried about her before we arrived in Potosi but she promised us that she will make sure to put on those much needed pounds before we head off again. So after our four-day stay and a good rest the three of us were ready to face new challenges and to continue with our journey.

Our first day passed without much hassle and after about 32km we arrived in a small town called Llacalle. Miss M found a corral for us but we were later moved to make place for the four donkeys who were supposed to stay there for the night. Miss M went round from house to house later that evening with one of the local guys to try and get us some Sebada (popular with cows) but it was all in vain as no-one was prepared to sell any to her… So we had to be satisfied with a bag of maize and avena that had arrived with one of the Flotas (long distance busses) which Miss M had arranged from Potosi to be delivered to a few villages on our way. Miss M found herself a big meeting room to sleep in and fortunately there was a spare matress she could use for the nights rest.

The following morning Miss M was told that our next village would be about 35km on but after 8 hours ride we were still in the middle of the mountains at an altitude of about 3800 metres with not a village or town in sight. Camping somewhere would have been impossible as well as there were no trees to which Miss M could tie us up. We were very lucky when we spotted later that afternoon a little settlement down in a valley which consisted of about 3 houses and corrals where they kept their Llamas. Miss M approached one of the men with a funny colourful traditional cap on his head and he agreed to let her have one of the corrals a bit away from the settlement. She bought a bundle of Sebada from him and as the rain started to come down combined with a strong wind, she began to pitch her tent a few metres away from our corral. After about an hour's struggle with the pitching, an out-of-breath (strenuous activity not easy at these high altitudes!) Miss M finally settled inside the tent with all the riding equipment and saddle-bags nestled at her feet. Just before dark she went to ask for hot water for her thermo and had to be satisfied with black tea and a few crackers for dinner. The night was a long and cold one (for all of us!) and when the sun threw its friendly face over the mountains early the following morning we slowly started to get ready with stiff and cold bones for another day's ride.

Our third day's ride after Potosi was a nightmare and Miss M had learned a precious lesson which is never to ask a local person the distance to the next destination - especially not in kilometres. More helpful would be to know how long it will take walking and then one can make the calculation as to what this would be riding…..Miss M had no breakfast that morning but she didn't worry too much as she was told that our next village would be about 5 kilometres on… this didn't turn out to be the case however and after a very frustrating 38km ride through mountains and valleys with hardly a person in sight we arrived at Cruce Ventilla late that afternoon.

At our next village, Tola Palca, Miss M met three cyclists who were cycling from Canada all the way down to Buenos Aires…. one of them even planned to go further and attempt a round-the-world trip with only two wheels as company. They invited Miss M to stay with them in a Casa de Salud, a two-roomed building which is used as a medical centre for the villagers who are in need of treatment or vaccinations. We were put up in a corral and Miss M paid a few local eager young girls to cut some dry grass for us. It was another freezing night with dust-storms first and later fierce winds and even snow. Mise and I lied down in the corral (Miss M cleared away all the stones for us earlier) but we both agreed the next morning that it had been the worst night for the two of us so far.

The only thing that kept us going at this stage was that La Paz was ahead and we knew that we would be able to get a good rest and proper food once we reach this capital city of Bolivia, 3-days ride from the Peruvian border.
